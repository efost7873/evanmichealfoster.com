# Research by Evan Foster

I write and conduct research on governance, financialization, public administration and organizational structure. My work focuses on what happens when institutions say certain things matter but never give them real authority, durable records or a place to stand inside the structure.

I am based in Springfield, Illinois and currently pursuing a Doctor of Public Administration at the University of Illinois Springfield. My doctoral research examines financialization and resource distribution in public institutions.

Alongside my academic work, I am the founder of [Integrated Value Architecture](https://integratedvaluearchitecture.com), a five-ledger governance architecture built to address a problem I kept seeing across sectors. Financial accounting usually has the clearest rules, the strongest records and the most durable authority inside an organization. Other forms of value may still be discussed, but they are easier to delay, harder to defend and more likely to be pushed through financial terms before they can carry real weight.

If you are searching for Evan Foster’s research, this page brings together my current research focus, working paper and external research profiles.

## Current Research Focus

My current research examines what happens when financial measures become the main authoritative ledger for organizational decision-making. When that happens, service quality, staffing capacity, long-term resilience, learning, equity and external impact do not disappear. They just lose ground more easily because they are not backed by the same structure.

That problem shows up across sectors. Public institutions, nonprofits and private organizations are often expected to protect multiple forms of value while still routing major decisions through one dominant accounting system. My research looks at what that does to resource distribution, decision-making, overload and the way institutions respond to pressure over time.

This work also informs the development of Integrated Value Architecture, a five-ledger governance architecture designed to give financial, operational, capacity, learning and innovation, and externalities and equity domains their own structural standing.
