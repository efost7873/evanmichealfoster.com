# About Evan Foster

I live and work in Springfield, Illinois. I am a founder, researcher and writer, and most of my work starts from the same basic frustration. Institutions usually do not fail because nobody cares or because people are not working hard enough. They fail because the structure routes authority badly, buries important work and makes it harder than it should be to act on what people can already see.

That way of thinking did not come from theory alone. It came from watching patterns early, usually before people were ready to say what was actually happening. I have always paid close attention to the gap between what organizations claim to value and what their structure actually protects. That instinct shaped how I moved through work long before I had cleaner language for it.

My early career started in the automotive industry, where I moved into finance and became a Finance Director at a car dealership in my early twenties. That gave me a direct view of how quickly distorted systems start to feel normal when they control the numbers, the decisions and the rewards. Later, I continued my education at the University of Illinois Springfield, where I earned a Bachelor of Science in Biology with an emphasis in pre-medicine and later a Master of Public Health. My path eventually moved into public health, environmental health leadership and complex organizational work.

That work led me to the American Lung Association, where I served as Senior Director of Healthy Air Solutions. In that role, I led national environmental health initiatives, worked across multiple states and managed major program and grant portfolios. I saw smart, capable people doing serious work inside structures that made it harder than it needed to be to act, coordinate, protect capacity or invest in what the organization would actually need later. Different mission. Different sector. Same underlying problem.

I also serve on the board of [350 Chicago](https://www.350chicago.org/meet-the-team), a nonprofit climate organization working across Chicago and Illinois. That role keeps me close to the practical side of nonprofit governance, environmental advocacy and mission-driven organizational work, and it strengthens the connection between my background in public health, my broader research interests and the structural questions that led me to build Integrated Value Architecture.

That is a large part of why I became a founder. I got tired of doing high-level work inside systems that moved too slowly, ignored good ideas, buried obvious problems and expected people to keep carrying more without ever fixing the structure underneath them. I did not want to keep handing my time, judgment and energy over to institutions that could name the problem and still dance around it. I wanted to build something real, on purpose, with enough freedom to follow the problem all the way down instead of stopping where the politics got uncomfortable.

That decision became [Integrated Value Architecture](https://integratedvaluearchitecture.com). IVA is a five-ledger governance architecture built to address the structural failure that develops when finance becomes the default home for every important decision, unresolved tradeoff and unowned form of value. I built it because too much important work keeps landing in finance's lap simply because finance is the only place in the organization with a formal ledger. Finance should be a governance partner. It should not be the default home for every form of value nobody else has formal authority to carry.

My work is professional, but it is personal too. I am a husband and father, and my family is a central reason I do this work. I care about building something that matters, but I also care about building a life that is stable, grounded and worth living. That changes how I think about time, work and what a serious life is supposed to add up to. I am not interested in drifting through institutions for another decade hoping the next one will waste my time a little less.

Place matters to me for the same reason. I grew up in Illinois, and Oregon, Illinois has remained one of the most important places in my life. It is where I got married, and it represents peace to me in a way very few places do. It reminds me that life does not have to be built around noise, drift or institutional theater. Structure stops being abstract pretty quickly once people have to live inside what it produces.

I was also raised in the LDS Church. I am less active than I once was, but that background still shaped the way I think about responsibility, institutional life, continuity and the obligations people carry to each other over time.

Alongside that work, I write and conduct research on governance, financialization and organizational structure. I am currently pursuing a Doctor of Public Administration at the University of Illinois Springfield, where my research examines how financialization shapes resource distribution and decision-making in public institutions. I am also developing a broader body of work that includes public writing, independent projects and my book project, [*Always Over Capacity*](/always-over-capacity/), which focuses on the structural causes of overload and the way important work keeps getting routed into the wrong places.

Across all of it, the common thread is pretty simple. I am interested in the point where what people know is happening and what the structure allows them to do stop matching. That is where overload starts. That is where value gets buried. That is where institutions begin asking people to carry problems that should have been solved in the design.
