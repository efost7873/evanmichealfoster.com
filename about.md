# About Evan Foster

I live and work in Springfield, Illinois, and I did not build this body of work by sitting far enough away from institutions that I could summarize them politely afterward. I built it by being inside enough of them to see what they do when authority gets routed badly, when the same few people end up carrying too much of the work, and when everybody can tell something is off but the structure still keeps forcing the problem through the wrong place. That is the thread running through almost everything I do now, whether it shows up as research, writing, board service, governance architecture or just me trying to say out loud what other people have usually already been complaining about in their offices for months.

My early career started in the automotive industry, where I moved into finance and became a Finance Director at a dealership in my early twenties. That gave me a direct view of what happens when the numbers do not just measure the environment but start governing it. I watched how quickly distorted incentives can start feeling normal once they control the money, the decisions and the rewards. Later, my path moved through biology, public health, healthcare-adjacent work, government and national nonprofit leadership, and the same structural pattern kept showing up in different forms. The paperwork changed. The titles changed. The loop did not.

That is a big part of why my path looks the way it does. I earned a Bachelor of Science in Biology with a pre-medicine emphasis and later a Master of Public Health from the University of Illinois Springfield. I am now in the Doctor of Public Administration program there, studying how federal tax policy reinforces financialization across sectors. Before UIS fully became the academic home, Lincoln Land Community College was where the academic side of my life started to stop feeling fake. I studied business administration there, was in the Honors Program, and joined Phi Theta Kappa. That was the first place where I stopped feeling like school was just another place I was proving what I could not do and started feeling like I could actually build something there. There is more on that on the [Academics](/academics/) page because it matters to the full story.

My professional work also includes national environmental health leadership and nonprofit board governance. I served in a senior national role at the American Lung Association, where I worked across large-scale environmental health and funding environments that kept sharpening the same basic question for me: why do organizations keep letting obvious structural problems sit in plain sight until the cost gets harder to ignore? I also serve as Board Treasurer of [350 Chicago](/350-chicago/), and that work matters because it keeps me close to the practical side of board governance, mission-driven work and financial oversight in a live organizational setting instead of just the theory of it.

That is also why I became a founder. I got tired of watching institutions name the problem, hire around the problem, schedule around the problem and talk around the problem without fixing the structure that kept producing it. I did not want to keep handing my time, judgment and energy over to systems that could see what was wrong and still keep routing authority badly enough that the same people ended up paying for it again. I wanted enough room to follow the pattern all the way down.

That decision became [Integrated Value Architecture](https://integratedvaluearchitecture.com). I built it because I kept seeing too much work and too much decision authority landing in the same few places, especially finance, not because finance was always the real problem but because finance was often the only place with formal standing, durable records and real decision force. The result was that service, capacity, learning, external effects and other forms of value kept getting pushed through a lane that was never built to carry all of them cleanly. IVA came out of watching that happen enough times to stop pretending it was a sector-specific issue.

My work is professional, but it is personal too. I am a husband and father, and that changes how I think about work, time and what a serious life is supposed to add up to. I am not interested in spending another decade inside institutions that know better and still keep wasting people. I care about building something that matters, but I also care about building something steady enough that my family does not have to live downstream from the same kind of structural drift I spend so much time naming in public.

Place matters to me for the same reason. I grew up in Illinois, and Oregon, Illinois remains one of the most important places in my life. It is where I got married, and it still represents peace to me in a way very few places do. Structure stops being abstract pretty quickly once you have to live inside what it produces, and place is one of the clearest ways I know to remember that.

I was also raised in the LDS Church. I am less active than I once was, but that background still shaped how I think about institutional life, continuity, responsibility and the obligations people carry to each other over time.

Across all of this, the common thread is simple. I am interested in the point where what people know is happening and what the structure allows them to do stop matching. That is where overload starts. That is where value gets buried. That is where organizations start asking people to carry problems that should have been solved in the design.
