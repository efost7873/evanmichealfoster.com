# About Evan Foster

I live and work in Springfield, Illinois. I am the Founder and Principal Architect of Integrated Value Architecture LLC, and I built the Integrated Value Architecture Standard because I got tired of seeing the same basic failure show up in every kind of organization I worked in. Important work kept getting delayed, buried or pushed into the wrong hands because only one domain in the organization had real standing. Finance had records, rules, decision rights and staying power. Service quality, staffing capacity, operational performance, learning, community impact and compliance exposure were all expected to matter, but usually without the same authority behind them. So decisions kept getting routed through finance even when the issue was operational, human or long term. That is how organizations overload good people, miss obvious problems and keep acting surprised when the same issues come back worse later.

I did not reach that conclusion from theory alone. I saw it from inside the work, and I usually saw it before people were ready to say it out loud. I have always paid close attention to patterns, incentives and the gap between what organizations say matters and what their structure actually protects. That instinct shaped my early career in the automotive industry, where I moved into finance and became a Finance Director at a car dealership in my early twenties. It gave me a direct view of how quickly people learn to treat a distorted system as normal when that system controls the numbers, the decisions and the rewards.

Later, I continued my education at the University of Illinois Springfield, where I earned a Bachelor of Science in Biology with an emphasis in pre-medicine and later a Master of Public Health. My path moved into public health, environmental health leadership and complex organizational work. That eventually led me to the American Lung Association, where I served as Senior Director of Healthy Air Solutions. In that role, I led national environmental health initiatives, worked across multiple states and managed major program and grant portfolios. I saw smart, capable people doing serious work inside structures that made it harder than it needed to be to act, coordinate, protect capacity or invest in what the organization would actually need six months from now.

I built IVA because too much important work keeps landing in finance’s lap simply because finance is the only place in the organization with a formal ledger. Finance should be a governance partner. It should not be the default home for every form of value, every unresolved tradeoff and every problem nobody else has formal authority to carry. When that happens, service quality gets treated as secondary, staffing gets stretched, learning gets postponed, compliance gets fragmented and external impact gets handled only when it becomes too visible to ignore.

IVA is a five-ledger governance architecture that gives financial, operational, capacity, learning and innovation, and externalities and equity domains their own structural standing. The point is not to weaken financial discipline. It is to stop forcing finance to carry work, accountability and decision pressure that should have been owned elsewhere from the start.

Founding my own company was not just a career move. I got tired of doing high-level work inside systems that moved too slowly, ignored good ideas, buried obvious problems and expected people to keep carrying more without ever fixing the structure underneath them. I wanted to build something real, on purpose, and I wanted the freedom to build it with my own judgment intact.

My work is professional, but it is personal too. I am a husband and father, and my family is a central reason I do this work. I care about building something that matters, but I also care about building a life that is stable, grounded and worth living. That has changed the way I think about time, risk, work and what a serious life is supposed to add up to. I care about work that holds up under real pressure because real people have to live inside the consequences when it does not.

Place matters to me for the same reason. I grew up in Illinois, and Oregon, Illinois has remained one of the most important places in my life. It is where I got married, and it represents peace to me in a way very few places do. It reminds me that life does not have to be built around noise, drift or institutional theater. Structure stops being abstract pretty quickly once people have to live inside what it produces.

I was also raised in the LDS Church. I am less active than I once was, but that background still shaped the way I think about responsibility, institutional life, continuity and the obligations people carry to each other over time.

Alongside that work, I write and conduct research on governance, financialization and organizational structure. I am currently pursuing a Doctor of Public Administration at the University of Illinois Springfield, where my research examines how financialization shapes resource distribution and decision-making in public institutions. My academic work and applied work are closely connected. One helps explain the pattern. The other is built to solve it.

I do this work for organizations that are tired of watching decisions slow down, work pile up and the things they say matter keep losing out because nobody has real authority to hold onto them. I continue to live and work in [Springfield, Illinois](/springfield/) while developing [Integrated Value Architecture](https://integratedvaluearchitecture.com), writing, conducting research and building governance architecture that helps organizations stop burying what they actually depend on.
