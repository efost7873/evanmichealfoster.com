# About Evan Foster

I live and work in Springfield, Illinois. I am the Founder and Principal Architect of Integrated Value Architecture LLC, and I built the Integrated Value Architecture Standard because I got tired of seeing the same failure show up in every kind of organization I worked in. Important work kept getting delayed, buried or pushed into the wrong hands because only one domain in the organization had real standing. Finance had records, rules, decision rights and staying power. Service quality, staffing capacity, operational performance, learning, community impact and compliance exposure were all expected to matter, but usually without the same authority behind them. So decisions kept getting routed through finance even when the issue was operational, human or long term. That is how organizations overload good people, miss obvious problems and keep acting surprised when the same issues come back worse six months later.

I did not reach that conclusion from theory alone. I saw it from inside the work. My early career started in the automotive industry, where I moved into finance and became a Finance Director at a car dealership in my early twenties. That gave me a direct view of how incentives shape behavior and how quickly people learn to treat a distorted system as normal when that system controls the numbers, the decisions and the rewards.

Later, I continued my education at the University of Illinois Springfield, where I earned a Bachelor of Science in Biology with an emphasis in pre-medicine and later a Master of Public Health. My path eventually moved into public health, environmental health leadership and complex organizational work. That path led me to the American Lung Association, where I served as Senior Director of Healthy Air Solutions. In that role, I led national environmental health initiatives, worked across multiple states and managed major program and grant portfolios. I saw smart, capable people doing serious work inside structures that made it harder than it needed to be to act, coordinate, protect capacity or invest in what the organization would actually need later.

I built IVA because too much important work keeps landing in finance’s lap simply because finance is the only place in the organization with a formal ledger. Finance should be a governance partner. It should not be the default home for every form of value, every unresolved tradeoff and every problem nobody else has formal authority to carry. When that happens, service quality gets treated as secondary, staffing gets stretched, learning gets postponed, compliance gets fragmented and external impact gets handled only when it becomes too visible to ignore.

IVA is a five-ledger governance architecture that gives financial, operational, capacity, learning and innovation, and externalities and equity domains their own structural standing. The point is not to weaken financial discipline. The point is to stop forcing finance to carry work, accountability and decision pressure that should have been owned elsewhere from the start.

Alongside that work, I write and conduct research on governance, financialization and organizational structure. I am currently pursuing a Doctor of Public Administration at the University of Illinois Springfield, where my research examines how financialization shapes resource distribution and decision-making in public institutions. My academic work and applied work are closely connected. One helps explain the pattern. The other is built to solve it.

My work is for organizations that are tired of watching decisions stall, overload spread and important work get treated like it matters right up until nobody has real authority to protect it. I continue to live and work in [Springfield, Illinois](/springfield/) while developing [Integrated Value Architecture](https://integratedvaluearchitecture.com), writing, conducting research and building governance architecture that helps organizations stop burying what they actually depend on.
