# About

I live and work in Springfield, Illinois. My work focuses on governance architecture and the structural conditions that shape how organizations recognize value and make decisions. The path that brought me here was not a straight one.

I grew up in Illinois and left high school during my senior year. As a teenager I made a series of bad decisions that eventually led to two years in the Illinois Department of Corrections youth facilities in Murphysboro and Harrisburg. That experience forced me to confront the consequences of my choices early and pushed me to take responsibility for the direction of my life.

When I returned home, work came first. I spent several years in the automotive industry, starting at the bottom and eventually working my way into finance. By my early twenties I had become a Finance Director at a car dealership, responsible for structuring vehicle financing and navigating the financial side of the business. That experience provided an unfiltered look at how incentives and institutional structures shape behavior inside organizations. It was also the point where I realized I did not want to spend my life operating inside a system that often rewarded the wrong things.

I left the automotive industry with the intention of pursuing medicine. Education became the path forward, and I eventually enrolled at the University of Illinois Springfield. I earned a Bachelor of Science in Biology and later a Master of Public Health from the University of Illinois Springfield. As family responsibilities grew, the long path of medical training no longer aligned with the kind of life I wanted to build, and my work gradually shifted toward public health and organizational systems.

That path eventually led me to the American Lung Association, where I served as Senior Director of Healthy Air Solutions. In that role I led national environmental health initiatives, worked across multiple states, and managed multimillion-dollar program portfolios.

Working inside large organizations exposed a pattern that appeared again and again. Decisions were often shaped less by the mission of the organization and more by the structural dominance of financial accounting systems. Forms of value that organizations clearly cared about—operational capacity, learning, long-term resilience, and external impact—rarely had the same structural authority as financial metrics.

Those observations eventually led me to develop Integrated Value Architecture, a governance framework designed to restore structural balance between different forms of organizational value. The framework introduces a multi-ledger governance architecture that allows financial, operational, capacity, learning, and external value domains to be recognized and governed within their own structures.

Alongside this work, I write and conduct research on governance architecture, financialization, and organizational structure. I am currently pursuing a Doctor of Public Administration at the University of Illinois Springfield, where my research examines financialization and resource distribution in public institutions.

I was also raised within the LDS Church. While my relationship with the church today is less active than it once was, that background played a role in shaping how I think about community, responsibility, and institutional life.

Two Springfield Herald News police blotter listings appear online when my name is searched. These pages simply reproduce daily arrest logs. In both cases involving me, no charges were filed and the matters did not proceed to prosecution. I did nothing wrong. I do not hide those records because they exist in the public domain, but they represent a very small part of a much longer life that includes rebuilding, education, public service, and the work I continue to pursue today.

Today I continue to live and work in Springfield, Illinois while developing Integrated Value Architecture and pursuing doctoral research at the University of Illinois Springfield.
