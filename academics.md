# Academics

My academic path did not start clean.

I left high school and earned a GED. I started in mechanical engineering at Southern Illinois University and left that program. By the time I got to Lincoln Land Community College, I had already given myself enough evidence to believe academics might not be where I was going to succeed.

Lincoln Land is where that shifted.

I studied business administration there for about two years, with a couple breaks in the middle. I was in the Honors Program and became a member of Phi Theta Kappa. That was the first place where school stopped feeling like something I was failing at and started feeling like something I could actually do.

Not in a motivational way. In a real way.

The work made sense. The expectations were clear. I could see what good looked like and I could meet it. That was new.

Lincoln Land still matters to me for that reason. It is part of my local connection, but more than that, it is where things turned.

## University of Illinois Springfield

After that, I went on to the University of Illinois Springfield and completed:

- **Master of Public Health**
- **Bachelor of Science in Biology**, with a pre-medicine emphasis

Those degrees matter, but not because they check a box.

Public health trained me to look at systems instead of stopping at surface explanations. Biology trained me to pay attention to mechanism instead of assumption. Both of those stayed with me.

## Doctoral Work

I am in the <a href="https://www.uis.edu/spmp/doctoral-students" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Doctor of Public Administration program at the University of Illinois Springfield</a>.

My dissertation focuses on how federal tax policy reinforces financialization across sectors.

That sounds academic because it is academic. The reason I care about it is not.

I’ve spent years inside organizations where decisions move away from the people doing the work, get run through someone farther from the problem, and come back down as more work for the same people to fix. I saw it in finance. I saw it in hospitals. I saw it in government. I saw it in nonprofits. The titles changed. The loop did not.

That is also part of what led me to build <a href="https://integratedvaluearchitecture.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Integrated Value Architecture</a>.

The academic side gives me a way to study those patterns directly. The rest comes from having lived inside them.

This page is not here to make that path look clean.

It is here because Lincoln Land was where things started to work, UIS is where I finished the degrees, and the doctoral work is where the same questions I’ve been carrying for years finally had a place to go.
